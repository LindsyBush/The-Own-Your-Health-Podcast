The recent passing of Betty Bayo, a Kenyan gospel singer and mother of two, left more than just a void in the music industry. It left two children with questions that no child should have to ask, and futures that now require protection from the very storms their mother would have shielded them from.

As a psychiatrist, I've sat across from too many grieving children who weren't just mourning a parent—they were mourning the loss of what could have been. The school fees that suddenly had no source. The therapy they needed but couldn't afford. The silent way childhood ends when adult responsibilities begin too soon.

Women, especially, carry hope like an invisible weight. We nurture futures in our minds while making daily sacrifices, believing our presence alone is the safety net. But love, however profound, does not pay school fees. It does not cover therapy sessions. It cannot materialize housing or food when the primary provider is gone.

Death is not the tragedy. Unpreparedness is.

The psychological impact of sudden loss on children isn't just about the immediate grief—it's about the unraveling of their world's predictability. When attachment figures disappear without systems in place, children don't just lose a person; they lose their fundamental sense of security.

This isn't about fear. It's about foresight. About recognizing that care extends beyond our physical presence. That true protection means planning for continuity even in our absence.

Dr. Linda Biomdo is a medical doctor who has seen grief not only in hospital wards, but in living rooms, classrooms, and futures altered too soon.
